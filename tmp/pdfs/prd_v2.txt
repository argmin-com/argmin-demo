1. Executive Summary
This document defines the product requirements for the Argmin Platform: an enterprise SaaS
product that provides application-level cost and usage attribution for AI workloads. The platform deploys inside
customer trust boundaries, constructs a graph-based attribution chain linking model invocations through services,
code ownership, identity, and organizational hierarchy to cost data, and delivers decision-enabling intelligence that no
incumbent category currently provides.
The product has three pillars: (1) cost attribution and observability, (2) model right-sizing and optimization
recommendations, and (3) a configurable policy engine operating in advisory and active modes. Environmental
impact (carbon attribution) is architecturally embedded as a fourth dimension but sequenced behind cost as the lead
value proposition and excluded from MVP scope. The patent architecture (v2.3, six-reviewer validated) provides the
technical foundation; this PRD translates that architecture into product requirements organized for implementation.
The document serves as the canonical reference for (a) validating the YC application demo against product scope,
(b) communicating product scope to technical co-founder candidates and CTO hires, (c) guiding design partner
conversations, and (d) providing the foundation for sprint planning once the engineering team is in place.

2. Product Vision and Positioning
2.1 Core Thesis
Workload-level AI cost visibility becomes infrastructure, not tooling. Three forcing functions drive this: (a) technical
compute variance (reasoning models consume 30 to 700x more compute than base models, making flat-rate
allocation meaningless), (b) economic governance pressure (CFOs and FinOps teams are beginning to demand
attribution as AI becomes a material line item), and (c) organizational decentralization (multiple teams adopting AI
independently with no cross-team visibility).

2.2 Positioning
The product is positioned as an AI Cost Control Layer: an optimizer, not a cost cop. Lead with model optimization for
engineering leadership (demonstrate that teams use expensive models for tasks where cheaper alternatives produce
equivalent quality), then expand to FinOps governance and CFO reporting after proving savings. Environmental
framing supports the founding narrative but does not lead product positioning.

2.3 Competitive Differentiation
• Decision-time intervention (pre-execution) versus post-hoc measurement. The platform intercepts AI
inference requests before they execute, rather than reporting on spend after the fact.
• Graph-based attribution without tags. Heuristic entity resolution across disconnected enterprise domains
(identity, CI/CD, source control, HR, billing) produces attribution where tagging-based approaches fail.
• Confidence-gated enforcement. Probabilistic attribution confidence directly gates decision-time actions;
low-confidence attributions cannot trigger irreversible runtime changes.
• Patent-defensible architecture. Provisional patent application (v2.3, six-reviewer validated) with two
independent claims and fifteen dependent claims covering the full pipeline from ingestion through decision-time
interception.

7. Functional Requirements: Decision-Time Interception
The decision-time interceptor is the primary differentiating component. It intercepts AI inference requests before
execution and enriches, gates, or redirects them based on attributed cost, confidence, and configured policies. The
interceptor never traverses the graph directly; it performs single-digit-millisecond index lookups with a fail-open circuit
breaker guaranteeing zero impact on application availability.

9. Functional Requirements: Interventions and Right-Sizing
Interventions are the product's primary mechanism for delivering measurable value. Each intervention is a
recommended action with estimated savings, confidence score, risk rating, and (where applicable) capability
equivalence verification status. Interventions are generated from attribution data, model intelligence, and policy
engine rules via a conditional rule-based system.

9.1 Optimization Methodology
The platform ships with a predefined set of optimization categories. For each category, the platform defines specific
data thresholds and variable conditions. As data is ingested from enabled integrations, it is parsed and stored in the
customer's isolated data environment. The platform continuously evaluates the parsed data against the predefined
rule set. When a specific combination of data thresholds is met (e.g., expensive model detected combined with
verified equivalent quality from a cheaper alternative for the same use case), the corresponding optimization category
is triggered, estimated savings are calculated, and the recommendation is presented. V1 operates on predefined
conditional rules with configurable thresholds. The rule set covers the most common and highest-impact optimization
patterns. Every recommendation traces to a specific rule and the data that triggered it.

10. Functional Requirements: Policy Engine
The policy engine provides configurable rules governing AI spend and deployment behavior. Policies operate at two
layers: Layer 2 (attribution-based detection, post-hoc) and Layer 3 (decision-time enforcement, real-time). The
engine's posture is optimizer, not cost cop: intervention recommendations are non-blocking by default, with active
blocking available as an explicit opt-in. In MVP, enforcement defaults to staging; production enforcement is opt-in
per-service and starts with static safety constraints (token size limits) before budget gates.

• CI/CD: GitHub Actions only. Jenkins is Phase 2.
• HR integration deferred. MVP uses flat org mapping or manual CSV import.

• Interception: Passive, Advisory, and Active-Lite modes. Full Active mode is Phase 2.
• Dashboard: Overview, Attribution (Models + Teams + Manual Mapping), Interventions, Cost Forecasting (with

• Policy engine: rule-based only. Adaptive/RL-based policies are Phase 3.
• Carbon: not in MVP. All FR-700 series requirements are P1/P2. Carbon data model architecturally present but
not exposed.
• Benchmarking: not in MVP. Deferred due to re-identification risk at small customer volumes.

• TRAC: MVP formula is Billed Cost + Confidence Risk Premium. Carbon Liability component deferred.
• Interceptor: Python/FastAPI. Compiled-language migration is post-Phase 1 if needed.
• Terminology: all user-facing instances of "waste" use "optimization potential" instead.

17.2 MVP Validation Criteria
Phase 1 succeeds if: (a) 2-3 design partners are actively using the product, (b) attribution coverage exceeds 70% of
each design partner's AI spend (as defined in Section 17.3), (c) at least one partner has acted on a right-sizing
recommendation with verified savings (before/after normalized by traffic volume, with rollback trigger if savings fail to
materialize), (d) at least one verbal commitment to convert to paid customer.

17.3 Attribution Coverage % Definition
Attribution Coverage % is the proportion of AI compute spend (USD) within supported ingestion providers during a
trailing 30-day window that the platform links to a terminal organizational node (team or cost center) with a confidence
score meeting or exceeding the chargeback threshold (default >= 0.80). Formula: Coverage % = (Attributed_Spend /
Total_Reconciled_Billed_Cost) x 100. Manual attribution inputs (FR-410) that confirm or correct attributions
contribute to Attributed_Spend when the resulting confidence meets the threshold. Spend attributed to fallback
buckets such as UNASSIGNED_TEAM or SHARED_PLATFORM does not count as terminal organizational nodes for
coverage purposes.

17.4 Deployment Topology (MVP)
All platform components deploy within the customer VPC on Kubernetes (EKS for MVP). Namespace isolation:
aci-ingestion, aci-core, aci-edge, aci-ui, aci-system. Optional vendor control plane (non-sensitive). Full deployment
topology, IAM requirements, network posture, and design partner installation milestones are documented in the
companion Engineering Specification v1.7.
